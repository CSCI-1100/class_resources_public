# The Connected World — Glossary of Terms

---

**2.4 GHz Band**
A Wi-Fi frequency band with longer range and better wall penetration, but lower maximum speeds. Shared with Bluetooth and other devices, making it more susceptible to interference.

**5G**
The fifth generation of cellular network technology. Significantly faster than 4G, with speeds potentially reaching 1 Gbps. Relies on a combination of infrastructure types including millimeter wave beacons in dense urban areas and larger cell towers in wider areas.

**5 GHz Band**
A Wi-Fi frequency band with higher maximum speeds but shorter range and more sensitivity to obstacles like walls and floors.

**Asymmetric Bandwidth**
An internet plan where download speed and upload speed are different (usually download is faster). Common in residential plans because most users download far more than they upload.

**Bandwidth**
The maximum amount of data that can be transmitted over a network connection in a given period of time. Typically measured in megabits per second (Mbps) or gigabits per second (Gbps).

**Buffering**
The process of preloading a portion of data before playback begins. Visible to users as a loading delay. Occurs when the incoming data stream is slower than the playback rate.

**Cell Tower**
A structure that houses antennas and equipment for transmitting and receiving cellular signals. 5G networks require more towers, placed closer together, than previous generations.

**Data Packet**
A small unit of data transmitted over a network. Large files and streams are broken into many packets, sent individually, and reassembled at the destination.

**DNS (Domain Name System)**
The system that translates human-readable domain names (like `google.com`) into IP addresses that computers can use to route traffic.

**Download Speed**
The rate at which data is received by your device from the internet. Relevant for streaming, browsing, and gaming.

**Encryption**
The process of scrambling data so that it can only be read by an authorized recipient who has the key to decrypt it.

**Ethernet**
A wired networking standard that connects devices to a network using a physical cable. Generally faster and more stable than Wi-Fi.

**HTTP (HyperText Transfer Protocol)**
The foundational protocol used for transmitting web pages over the internet.

**HTTPS (HyperText Transfer Protocol Secure)**
A secure version of HTTP that encrypts data in transit. Used for secure web browsing, logins, and online transactions. Identified by the padlock icon in a browser.

**IP Address (Internet Protocol Address)**
A unique numerical label assigned to each device on a network. Used to identify the source and destination of data packets.

**ISP (Internet Service Provider)**
The company that provides your connection to the internet (e.g., Comcast, AT&T, Brightridge). The ISP assigns your router a public IP address and routes your traffic to and from the wider internet.

**Latency**
The delay between sending a request and receiving a response. High latency causes lag. Related to ping; the terms are often used interchangeably.

**Local IP Address**
An IP address assigned to a device within a private network (e.g., your home). Not visible or accessible from the public internet. Typically follows the format `192.168.x.x`.

**Millimeter Wave (mmWave)**
A high-frequency radio band used by some 5G deployments. Offers very high speeds but limited range and poor penetration of obstacles. Requires dense infrastructure (beacons on nearly every block) to be effective.

**Modem**
Short for *modulator-demodulator*. The device that connects your router to your Internet Service Provider (ISP). It translates signals between the format used inside your home and the format used by the ISP's network. In lecture, I obscured this by combining it with the role of the router.

**Packet Loss**
When one or more data packets fail to reach their destination. Can result in errors, dropped connections, or the need to resend data.

**Ping**
The time it takes for a data packet to travel from your device to a server and back. Measured in milliseconds (ms). A lower ping means a more responsive connection — critical for online gaming.

**Protocol**
A standardized set of rules that defines how data is formatted, transmitted, and received between devices or systems.

**Public IP Address**
The IP address assigned to your router by your ISP. This is the address that servers on the internet use to send data back to your home network.

**Router**
A device that connects your local network to the internet and directs (routes) data packets between devices. It manages traffic by tracking which device sent which request and delivering responses back to the correct recipient.

**Server**
A powerful computer (or cluster of computers) that stores websites, applications, and data, and responds to requests from client devices. Large services like Google distribute this load across many servers worldwide.

**SMTP (Simple Mail Transfer Protocol)**
The standard protocol used for sending emails over the internet.

**Streaming**
A method of transmitting data (video, audio) in a continuous flow of packets so that playback can begin before the entire file is downloaded.

**Switch**
A device that connects multiple computers within a local network, allowing them to communicate with each other. Unlike a router, a switch does not connect to the internet — it manages traffic within the local network only.

**Symmetric Bandwidth**
An internet plan where download and upload speeds are equal.

**Throttling**
When an ISP intentionally reduces a customer's internet speed, often during peak usage periods or after a data usage threshold is reached.

**Upload Speed**
The rate at which data is sent from your device to the internet. Relevant for video calls, file sharing, and sending requests.

**Wi-Fi**
A wireless networking technology that allows devices to connect to a local network and the internet without physical cables. Operates over radio frequency bands.
