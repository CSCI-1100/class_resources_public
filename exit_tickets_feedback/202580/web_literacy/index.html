üç™ Cookies & Online Privacy

What are cookies and how do they work? ‚ñ∂

Cookies are small text files that websites store on your device to remember information about you.

Think of cookies like a coat check ticket:

  • You give the restaurant your coat and get a ticket with a number
  • When you return, you show the ticket and get your specific coat back
  • Similarly, a website gives your browser a "ticket" (cookie) so it remembers you next time

üìù What Cookies Store

  • Login status (so you stay signed in)
  • Shopping cart items
  • Site preferences (language, theme)
  • Browsing history on that site

üîÑ How They Work

  1. You visit a website
  2. Website sends a cookie to your browser
  3. Browser stores the cookie
  4. Next time you visit, browser sends cookie back
  5. Website recognizes you!
Important: Cookies are NOT viruses or programs. They're just text files that store information. They can't access your computer or steal passwords directly.
Is it safe to accept cookies? Which ones should I be careful about? ‚ñ∂

Not all cookies are created equal! Here's what you need to know:

‚úÖ Generally Safe: First-Party Cookies

What they are: Created by the website you're actually visiting

Purpose: Keep you logged in, remember your cart, save preferences

Privacy risk: Low - only that website can see this data

Verdict: Usually safe to accept

‚ö†Ô∏è Be Cautious: Third-Party Cookies

What they are: Created by advertisers and other companies, not the site you're on

Purpose: Track you across multiple websites for advertising

Privacy risk: Higher - builds profile of your browsing habits

Verdict: Consider blocking these

Pro tip: Most browsers now let you block third-party cookies while keeping first-party cookies. This is a good balance between functionality and privacy!

Red flags - reject cookies when:

  • You're on a sketchy or unfamiliar website
  • The site requests excessive permissions
  • You're using a public/shared computer
  • The cookie policy is vague or concerning
How do I delete cookies? How do I know if third-party cookies are blocked? ‚ñ∂

Every browser has built-in tools for managing cookies. Here's how:

Chrome

  1. Delete cookies: Settings ‚Üí Privacy and security ‚Üí Clear browsing data ‚Üí Check "Cookies" ‚Üí Clear data
  2. Block third-party cookies: Settings ‚Üí Privacy and security ‚Üí Third-party cookies ‚Üí Select "Block third-party cookies"
  3. Check if blocked: Look for the cookie icon in the address bar when visiting sites

Firefox

  1. Delete cookies: Settings ‚Üí Privacy & Security ‚Üí Cookies and Site Data ‚Üí Clear Data
  2. Block third-party cookies: Settings ‚Üí Privacy & Security ‚Üí Enhanced Tracking Protection ‚Üí Choose "Strict"
  3. Check if blocked: Click the shield icon in the address bar

Safari

  1. Delete cookies: Preferences ‚Üí Privacy ‚Üí Manage Website Data ‚Üí Remove All
  2. Block third-party cookies: Preferences ‚Üí Privacy ‚Üí Check "Prevent cross-site tracking"
  3. Check if blocked: Safari blocks by default; check Privacy settings to confirm
Heads up: Deleting cookies will log you out of websites and clear your preferences. You'll need to sign in again.
Do cookies follow me from app to app on my phone? Is app tracking the same as cookies? ‚ñ∂

This is a great question! Cookies and app tracking are related but different:

üç™ Web Cookies

Where: Web browsers (Safari, Chrome)

How they work: Stored by websites in your browser

Scope: Limited to web browsing

Control: Clear through browser settings

üì± App Tracking

Where: Mobile apps (Instagram, TikTok, games)

How they work: Uses device identifiers (IDFA on iPhone, AAID on Android)

Scope: Can track across multiple apps

Control: Managed through phone privacy settings

The short answer: Traditional cookies don't follow you from app to app, BUT apps use similar tracking technologies that DO follow you across apps.

How to Control App Tracking:

  1. iPhone (iOS 14.5+): Settings ‚Üí Privacy & Security ‚Üí Tracking ‚Üí Turn off "Allow Apps to Request to Track"
  2. Android: Settings ‚Üí Privacy ‚Üí Ads ‚Üí Delete advertising ID or opt out of personalization
  3. Per-app basis: When an app asks "Allow [App] to track you?", tap "Ask App Not to Track"
That "creepy" feeling when ads follow you? That's often app tracking and third-party cookies working together to build a profile of your interests across devices and platforms.
Which types of cookies are most dangerous? ‚ñ∂

Let's rank cookies by privacy risk:

üü¢ Low Risk: Session Cookies

Lifespan: Deleted when you close browser

Purpose: Keep you logged in during visit

Risk level: Very low

üü° Medium Risk: Persistent First-Party Cookies

Lifespan: Days to years

Purpose: Remember preferences, keep you logged in

Risk level: Low to medium

üü† Higher Risk: Third-Party Tracking Cookies

Lifespan: Months to years

Purpose: Track browsing across websites

Risk level: Medium to high

üî¥ Highest Risk: Zombie/Evercookies

Lifespan: Very hard to delete

Purpose: Recreate themselves after deletion

Risk level: High (rarely used legally)

The real danger isn't the cookie itself - it's what companies do with the data. Third-party cookies can create detailed profiles of your interests, habits, health concerns, and more by tracking you across thousands of websites.

Why are there no legal protections? Laws are catching up slowly. The EU has GDPR (requiring cookie consent), California has CCPA, but federal US privacy laws are limited. Companies lobby heavily against stricter cookie regulations: $21 million in 2022 toward anti-data anti-data privacy law according to Birnbaum (2022) as cited by Lemons (2025), an informative source for understanding why the US lags behind.

What's the difference between the Internet and the Web? ‚ñ∂

These terms are often used interchangeably, but they're different!

üîå The Internet

What it is: The physical network infrastructure

Think of it as: The roads and highways

Includes: Cables, routers, satellites, cell towers

Purpose: Connects billions of devices worldwide

Existed since: 1960s (ARPANET)

üåê The Web (World Wide Web)

What it is: A system of linked documents and pages

Think of it as: The cars traveling on those roads

Includes: Websites, web pages, hyperlinks

Purpose: Share and access information through browsers

Existed since: 1989 (invented by Tim Berners-Lee)

Analogy: The Internet is like the postal system (infrastructure), and the Web is like the letters and packages (content) that travel through it.

Other things that use the Internet but aren't the Web:

  • Email (uses SMTP protocol, not HTTP)
  • Online gaming
  • Video calls (Zoom, FaceTime)
  • File sharing (torrents)
  • Internet of Things (smart devices)
How do web servers work? I'm confused about servers, especially Google's servers. ‚ñ∂

A web server is just a powerful computer that stores websites and sends them to your browser when requested.

What Happens When You Visit a Website:

  1. You type URL: You enter "google.com" in your browser
  2. DNS lookup: Your computer asks "What's the address of google.com?" (gets an IP address like 142.250.80.46)
  3. Request sent: Your browser sends a request to Google's server at that address
  4. Server processes: Google's server receives your request and prepares the webpage
  5. Response sent back: Server sends HTML, CSS, images back to your browser
  6. Browser displays: Your browser assembles and displays the webpage

What makes Google's servers special:

  • Scale: Google has millions of servers in data centers worldwide
  • Speed: They use nearby servers (edge computing) so responses are fast
  • Redundancy: If one server fails, another takes over instantly
  • Distribution: Your search might be handled by servers in multiple countries simultaneously
Fun fact: When you search on Google, your query might be processed by servers on multiple continents in milliseconds, with results aggregated and sent back to you almost instantly!
Tell me more about those undersea cables! ‚ñ∂

Undersea cables are the backbone of the global Internet - and they're fascinating!

üìä By The Numbers

  • Over 500 undersea cables worldwide
  • 1.3+ million km total length
  • 99% of intercontinental data travels through them
  • Fiber optic technology - faster than satellites

üîß How They're Made

  • About as thick as a garden hose
  • Multiple layers of protection (steel, copper, plastic)
  • Contain fiber optic strands thinner than human hair
  • Can transmit terabits per second

How they get there:

  • Special ships called cable-laying vessels slowly lay cable on ocean floor
  • Takes months to lay a single transatlantic cable
  • Cables follow seafloor topography to avoid damage
  • Cost hundreds of millions of dollars per cable
Want to see them? Check out submarinecablemap.com for an interactive map of all undersea Internet cables!

Why not satellites? Satellites have much higher latency (delay) because signals have to travel to space and back. Cables are much faster for most Internet traffic.

What are HTML, CSS, and JavaScript? I'm confused about their roles. ‚ñ∂

Think of building a website like building a house:

üèóÔ∏è HTML (HyperText Markup Language)

Role: The structure/skeleton

Like: The walls, roof, doors, and rooms of a house

Creates: Headings, paragraphs, images, links, buttons

Example: "Put a heading here, then a paragraph, then an image"

üé® CSS (Cascading Style Sheets)

Role: The styling/appearance

Like: Paint colors, furniture, decorations

Creates: Colors, fonts, layouts, spacing, animations

Example: "Make the heading blue and centered"

‚ö° JavaScript

Role: The interactivity/behavior

Like: Lights that turn on, doors that open, AC that adjusts

Creates: Clickable buttons, form validation, dynamic content

Example: "When button clicked, show a message"

<button>Click Me</button> /* CSS: Style */ button { background-color: blue; color: white; padding: 10px; } // JavaScript: Behavior button.addEventListener('click', function() { alert('Hello!'); });
Remember: HTML = What content appears, CSS = How it looks, JavaScript = How it behaves.
I'm confused about how to actually create a website. Where do I start? ‚ñ∂

Creating your first website is easier than you think! Here's a beginner-friendly approach:

Method 1: Online Editor (Easiest - Start Here!)

  1. Go to: CodePen.io or JSFiddle.net
  2. Create free account: Takes 30 seconds
  3. Click "New Pen": You'll see three panels (HTML, CSS, JS)
  4. Type in HTML panel:
    <h1>My First Website</h1> <p>Hello, world!</p>
  5. See it instantly: Your website appears in the preview panel!
  6. Add style in CSS panel:
    h1 { color: blue; } p { font-size: 20px; }

Method 2: On Your Computer

  1. Create a folder: Name it "my-website"
  2. Open a text editor: Notepad (Windows), TextEdit (Mac), or VS Code
  3. Create a new file: Name it "index.html"
  4. Type this basic template:
    <!DOCTYPE html> <html> <head> <title>My Website</title> </head> <body> <h1>Welcome to My Website</h1> <p>This is my first webpage!</p> </body> </html>
  5. Save the file
  6. Open in browser: Double-click the file or drag it into a browser
Pro tip: Start with online editors like CodePen to learn. Once comfortable, move to creating files on your computer for more control.
How do I switch between HTML and CSS in the same file? Can they be in one file? ‚ñ∂

Great question! You can include CSS in the same HTML file or keep them separate. Here's how:

Option 1: CSS Inside HTML (Same File)

<!DOCTYPE html> <html> <head> <title>My Page</title> <style> /* CSS goes here between style tags */ h1 { color: blue; } p { font-size: 18px; } </style> </head> <body> <!-- HTML content here --> <h1>Hello World</h1> <p>This is a paragraph.</p> </body> </html>

Use when: Small projects, quick tests

Option 2: Separate CSS File (Better Practice)

File: index.html

<!DOCTYPE html> <html> <head> <title>My Page</title> <link rel="stylesheet" href="styles.css"> </head> <body> <h1>Hello World</h1> <p>This is a paragraph.</p> </body> </html>

File: styles.css

h1 { color: blue; } p { font-size: 18px; }

Use when: Larger projects, professional work

You don't "switch" between them - HTML contains the content structure, and CSS (whether in the same file or separate) defines how it looks. They work together simultaneously!
I didn't know CSS existed! Can you explain it more? ‚ñ∂

CSS is what makes websites beautiful! Without it, every website would look like a plain document.

What CSS can do:

  • Colors: Change text, backgrounds, borders
  • Fonts: Change size, weight, family (Arial, Times, etc.)
  • Layout: Position elements, create columns
  • Spacing: Add margins and padding
  • Effects: Shadows, rounded corners, transparency
  • Animations: Make things move and transition
  • Responsive design: Make sites work on phones, tablets, desktops

Basic CSS Syntax

selector { property: value; } /* Example: */ h1 { color: red; font-size: 32px; text-align: center; }

Real Example

/* Make all buttons blue */ button { background-color: blue; color: white; padding: 10px 20px; border: none; border-radius: 5px; } /* When hover over button */ button:hover { background-color: darkblue; }
Try it out: Go to any website, right-click, select "Inspect", and you can see and edit the CSS in real-time! Changes are temporary and only on your screen.
How were people making websites before Wix and other website builders? ‚ñ∂

Before drag-and-drop builders, people coded websites by hand - and many still do!

Evolution of Web Development:

  1. 1990s - Pure HTML: People wrote HTML in text editors, uploaded via FTP. Sites were simple, mostly text and links.
  2. Late 1990s - HTML + CSS: CSS was introduced to separate content from design. Tables were used for layout.
  3. Early 2000s - Dynamic Sites: PHP, JavaScript made sites interactive. Content Management Systems (WordPress, Joomla) emerged.
  4. 2010s - Modern Frameworks: Bootstrap, React, responsive design. Professional tools like Dreamweaver.
  5. Today: Both methods coexist - builders (Wix, Squarespace) for simplicity, hand-coding for customization.

Why some people still code by hand:

  • More control: Can do exactly what you want
  • Better performance: Cleaner, faster code
  • No monthly fees: Website builders charge subscription fees
  • Unique designs: Not limited to templates
  • Learning: Understanding how websites work
The truth: Website builders like Wix are great for quick, simple sites. Hand-coding is better for complex, custom applications. Many professionals use a mix of both approaches!

Can you break down the anatomy of a URL? I'm still confused. ‚ñ∂

A URL is like an address for web content. Let's break down each part:

https://www.example.com:443/folder/page.html?id=123&sort=asc#section2 |___| |_| |_________| |_| |________________| |_____________| |_______| | | | | | | | Protocol | Domain Port Path Query Fragment Subdomain String

üîí Protocol (https://)

What it is: The method of communication

HTTP: HyperText Transfer Protocol (not secure)

HTTPS: HTTP Secure (encrypted - look for lock icon)

Why it matters: HTTPS protects your data from hackers

üåê Domain (example.com)

What it is: The human-readable name for the website

Parts: "example" (name) + ".com" (extension)

Behind the scenes: Points to an IP address

Example: google.com ‚Üí 142.250.80.46

üìÅ Subdomain (www.)

What it is: A subdivision of the main domain

www: Most common, stands for "World Wide Web"

Other examples: mail.google.com, blog.example.com

Purpose: Organize different sections or services

üìÇ Path (/folder/page.html)

What it is: Location of specific file on server

Like: Folder structure on your computer

Example: /products/shoes/nike.html

‚ùì Query String (?id=123&sort=asc)

What it is: Additional data sent to the server

Starts with: ? (question mark)

Format: key=value pairs separated by &

Example: Search results, filters, tracking

üîñ Fragment (#section2)

What it is: Jumps to specific part of page

Starts with: # (hash/pound sign)

Example: #comments scrolls to comments section

Note: Not sent to server, handled by browser

Memory trick: Read a URL left-to-right like giving directions: "Use secure protocol, go to this website, enter this folder, open this file, use these settings, and scroll to this section."
What does the "S" stand for in HTTPS? ‚ñ∂

The "S" stands for "Secure" - and it's very important!

üîì HTTP (Without S)

Security: None - data sent in plain text

Anyone can: Read your passwords, credit cards, messages

Vulnerable to: Eavesdropping, man-in-the-middle attacks

Browser warning: "Not Secure" in address bar

üîí HTTPS (With S)

Security: Encrypted communication

Protection: Data scrambled, unreadable to hackers

Safe for: Passwords, shopping, banking, personal info

Browser shows: Lock icon üîí in address bar

How HTTPS encryption works:

  1. Your browser asks the website for its security certificate
  2. Website provides certificate (proves it's legitimate)
  3. Browser and website agree on encryption method
  4. All data is now scrambled before sending
  5. Only your browser and the website can unscramble it
Never enter sensitive information on HTTP sites! This includes passwords, credit card numbers, social security numbers, or personal details. Always look for the lock icon.
Good news: As of 2024, more than\ 80% of web pages were loaded using HTTPS. Most modern browsers warn you loudly if you try to visit an HTTP site.
What are subdomains? Why do some sites have "www" and others don't? ‚ñ∂

Subdomains are subdivisions of a main domain, used to organize different parts of a website or service.

Common subdomain examples:

  • www.example.com - Main website (traditional)
  • mail.google.com - Email service
  • maps.google.com - Maps service
  • blog.company.com - Company blog
  • shop.company.com - Online store
  • api.service.com - Developer API
  • m.site.com - Mobile version (older sites)

About "www":

Historical Context

In the early web, "www" distinguished web servers from mail servers (mail.example.com) or FTP servers (ftp.example.com). It was a convention that stuck.

Modern Practice

Today, "www" is optional. Both example.com and www.example.com typically work. Sites usually redirect one to the other for consistency.

Try it: Visit google.com and www.google.com - they both work! Most sites automatically redirect to their preferred version.

Why use subdomains:

  • Organization: Separate different services
  • Technical reasons: Different servers, different technologies
  • Testing: dev.example.com for development
  • Localization: uk.example.com, fr.example.com
What is a query string and why is it confusing? ‚ñ∂

Query strings are the "?" part of URLs that pass information to websites. They look complex but follow simple rules.

https://shop.com/search?product=laptop&price=500&color=silver&sort=price Breaking it down: ? = Start of query string product=laptop = First parameter (key=value) & = Separator between parameters price=500 = Second parameter & = Another separator color=silver = Third parameter & = Another separator sort=price = Fourth parameter

Real-world examples you use every day:

  • Google search: google.com/search?q=cute+cats
  • YouTube video: youtube.com/watch?v=dQw4w9WgXcQ
  • Amazon filters: amazon.com/s?k=headphones&rh=p_36:0-5000
  • Tracking links: site.com/page?utm_source=email&utm_campaign=summer

What Query Strings Do

  • Pass search terms
  • Apply filters (price range, size, color)
  • Track where you came from (marketing)
  • Set preferences (language, items per page)
  • Identify specific content (video ID, product ID)

Rules of Query Strings

  • Always start with ?
  • Format: key=value
  • Multiple parameters separated by &
  • Spaces become + or %20
  • Special characters are encoded
Privacy note: Query strings are visible in your browser history and can be logged by servers. Don't put sensitive information (like passwords) in query strings!
Experiment: When you search on Google or filter products on Amazon, watch the URL change. You're seeing query strings in action!
