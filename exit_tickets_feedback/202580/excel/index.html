üßÆ Formulas and Data Manipulation

‚ñº
I'm still confused about how to use formulas. Where do I even start? ‚ñ∂

Don't worry - formulas seem intimidating at first, but they follow simple patterns once you understand the basics!

The Golden Rule: All Excel formulas start with an equals sign (=). If you don't type =, Excel treats it as text, not a calculation.

How to Write Your First Formula:

  1. Click the cell where you want the result to appear
  2. Type the equals sign: =
  3. Type your formula (we'll cover examples below)
  4. Press Enter - Excel calculates the result!

üìù Basic Math Operators

  • + Addition: =5+3 ‚Üí 8
  • - Subtraction: =10-4 ‚Üí 6
  • * Multiplication: =6*7 ‚Üí 42
  • / Division: =20/4 ‚Üí 5
  • ^ Exponent: =2^3 ‚Üí 8

üéØ Using Cell References

Instead of typing numbers, reference cells:

=A1+B1

This adds whatever is in cell A1 to whatever is in B1

Why this is powerful: Change A1 or B1, and the result updates automatically!

Pro tip: Instead of typing cell references, you can click on the cells while writing your formula. Excel will insert the reference for you!
What are the most important formulas I need to know? ‚ñ∂

Here are the "Big 5" formulas that cover 80% of what most people need in Excel:

1Ô∏è‚É£ SUM - Add numbers together

What it does: Adds up a range of numbers

=SUM(A1:A10)

Adds all numbers from A1 through A10

Example use: Total sales, total expenses, grade totals

2Ô∏è‚É£ AVERAGE - Find the mean

What it does: Calculates the average of numbers

=AVERAGE(B1:B20)

Finds the average of B1 through B20

Example use: Average test score, average temperature, mean salary

3Ô∏è‚É£ COUNT - Count cells with numbers

What it does: Counts how many cells contain numbers

=COUNT(C1:C50)

Counts cells with numbers in C1 through C50

Example use: How many students took the test, how many sales were made

4Ô∏è‚É£ IF - Make decisions

What it does: Returns different values based on a condition

=IF(D1>70, "Pass", "Fail")

If D1 is greater than 70, shows "Pass", otherwise "Fail"

Example use: Pass/fail grades, discount eligibility, status labels

5Ô∏è‚É£ VLOOKUP - Find information

What it does: Looks up information in a table

=VLOOKUP(E1, A1:C100, 2, FALSE)

Finds E1 in the first column of A1:C100, returns value from 2nd column

Example use: Find student names from ID numbers, look up prices from product codes

Common mistake: Forgetting to include the equals sign (=) at the beginning. Without it, Excel won't calculate anything!
What's the difference between relative and absolute cell references? When do I use $ signs? ‚ñ∂

This is one of the most powerful (and confusing) concepts in Excel. Let me break it down:

üìç Relative Reference (A1)

What it is: Cell reference that changes when you copy the formula

=A1*2

When you copy down:

  • Row 1: =A1*2
  • Row 2: =A2*2 (changed!)
  • Row 3: =A3*2 (changed!)

Use when: You want the formula to adjust to each row/column

üîí Absolute Reference ($A$1)

What it is: Cell reference that NEVER changes when copied

=A1*$B$1

When you copy down:

  • Row 1: =A1*$B$1
  • Row 2: =A2*$B$1 (B1 stays same!)
  • Row 3: =A3*$B$1 (B1 stays same!)

Use when: You want to reference one fixed cell (like a tax rate or conversion factor)

Real-World Example: Calculating Prices with Tax

  1. Put your tax rate in cell B1: 0.07 (7% tax)
  2. Put prices in column A (A3, A4, A5, etc.)
  3. In B3, type:
    =A3*(1+$B$1)
  4. Copy the formula down - A3 changes (A4, A5...) but $B$1 stays locked!
Quick tip: To add dollar signs quickly, click on the cell reference in your formula and press F4. It cycles through: A1 ‚Üí $A$1 ‚Üí A$1 ‚Üí $A1
Mixed references exist too:
  • $A1 - Column locked, row changes
  • A$1 - Row locked, column changes
These are useful for more advanced scenarios!
How do I apply a formula to an entire column without typing it over and over? ‚ñ∂

There are several quick ways to copy formulas down a column - no tedious retyping required!

Method 1: The Fill Handle (Easiest)

  1. Type your formula in the first cell (e.g., B2)
  2. Press Enter - your formula calculates
  3. Click back on that cell to select it
  4. Look for the small square in the bottom-right corner of the cell (the "fill handle")
  5. Hover over it until your cursor becomes a black crosshair (+)
  6. Click and drag down as far as you need
  7. Release - Excel copies the formula to all selected cells!

Method 2: Double-Click Fill (Fastest for Large Datasets)

  1. Type your formula in the first cell
  2. Select that cell
  3. Move to the fill handle (bottom-right corner)
  4. Double-click the fill handle
  5. Excel automatically fills down to match adjacent data!

Method 3: Copy-Paste

  1. Select the cell with your formula
  2. Copy it (Ctrl+C or Cmd+C)
  3. Select the range where you want the formula
  4. Paste (Ctrl+V or Cmd+V)
Pro tip: The double-click fill method (Method 2) is a game-changer for large spreadsheets with thousands of rows!
Watch out: Make sure you're using the right type of cell reference (relative vs. absolute) before copying formulas, or your calculations might reference the wrong cells!

üìä Pivot Tables and Charts

‚ñº
What's the difference between a PivotTable and a PivotChart? Which should I create? ‚ñ∂

This is one of the most confusing aspects for students! Let me clarify the difference:

üìä PivotTable

What it is: A data summary table with rows and columns of numbers

Looks like: A spreadsheet grid with calculated totals

Best for: Detailed numerical analysis, seeing exact values

Example output: A table showing each salesperson's name with their total sales amount

üìà PivotChart

What it is: A visual chart (bar, line, pie) that summarizes data

Looks like: A colorful graph with bars, lines, or pie slices

Best for: Visual presentation, spotting trends, comparing categories at a glance

Example output: A bar chart showing each salesperson with a colored bar representing their sales

KEY POINT: When your assignment asks for a "PivotChart," you should create a CHART (visual graph), NOT a table of numbers. Many students create a PivotTable by mistake!
The relationship: Every PivotChart is based on a PivotTable (Excel creates one automatically behind the scenes), but not every PivotTable needs a PivotChart. If your lab asks for a PivotChart, you're being asked for the visual graph!

Step-by-Step: Creating a PivotChart (What You Need for Labs!)

  1. Prepare your data:
    • Make sure your data has headers in the first row (e.g., "Date", "Name", "Sales")
    • No blank rows or columns in the middle of your data
    • Each column contains one type of information
  2. Select your data: Click any cell within your data range
  3. Insert the PivotChart:
    • Windows: Insert tab ‚Üí PivotChart button (or PivotChart dropdown ‚Üí PivotChart)
    • Mac: Insert tab ‚Üí PivotChart button
    • IMPORTANT: Click "PivotChart" NOT "PivotTable"!
  4. Choose chart location: Dialog box appears - usually choose "New Worksheet"
  5. Click OK: Excel creates BOTH a chart AND a pivot table (the table is hidden behind the scenes or in a separate area)
  6. Build your PivotChart: You'll see the chart area and a field list on the right. Drag fields:
    • Axis (Categories): What you want along the bottom (e.g., Salesperson names, Months)
    • Legend (Series): What you want to compare with colors (optional)
    • Values: What you want to measure (e.g., Sales Amount - creates bar heights)
    • Filters: What you want to filter by (e.g., Year, Region)
  7. Choose chart type: Right-click chart ‚Üí Change Chart Type if you want a different style (bar, line, pie, etc.)
Pro tip: You can tell if you created the right thing by looking at what appears on your screen:
  • ‚úÖ PivotChart: You see a colorful visual chart/graph
  • ‚ùå PivotTable: You see a grid of numbers and text (no visual bars/lines)

üéØ Quick PivotChart Example

Goal: Bar chart showing total sales by salesperson

  1. Select your data
  2. Insert ‚Üí PivotChart
  3. Drag "Salesperson" to Axis
  4. Drag "Sales Amount" to Values
  5. You should see colorful bars!

‚ùå Common Lab Mistake

Assignment says: "Create a PivotChart"

Student does: Insert ‚Üí PivotTable

Result: They get a table of numbers, not a chart ‚ùå

Correct action: Insert ‚Üí PivotChart

Result: They get a visual chart ‚úÖ

How do I create charts in Excel? And how do I make pivot charts? ‚ñ∂

Excel offers two types of charts: regular charts and pivot charts. Let's cover both!

Method 1: Regular Charts (From Any Data)

  1. Select your data: Click and drag to highlight the data you want to chart (include headers)
  2. Go to Insert tab
  3. Choose a chart type:
    • Column/Bar: Compare values across categories
    • Line: Show trends over time
    • Pie: Show parts of a whole
    • Scatter: Show relationship between two variables
  4. Click your choice: Excel inserts the chart immediately!
  5. Customize: Click on chart elements to change titles, colors, labels

Method 2: Pivot Charts (From Pivot Tables)

  1. Create or select your pivot table first
  2. Click anywhere in the pivot table
  3. Go to Insert tab ‚Üí PivotChart (or on PivotTable Analyze tab)
  4. Choose chart type
  5. Click OK

üìä Regular Charts

Pros:

  • Simple and straightforward
  • Good for static presentations
  • Easy to format

Cons:

  • Not interactive
  • Need to recreate if data structure changes

üéØ Pivot Charts

Pros:

  • Interactive - has filter buttons
  • Updates automatically with pivot table
  • Can quickly change what you're viewing

Cons:

  • Slightly more complex
  • Requires understanding pivot tables first
Which chart type should I use?
  • Comparing categories: Column or Bar chart
  • Showing change over time: Line chart
  • Showing percentages of a whole: Pie or Donut chart
  • Showing correlation: Scatter plot
Quick tip: Not sure which chart works best? Select your data and press Alt + F1 (Windows) or Fn + Option + F1 (Mac) to instantly create a default chart. You can always change the type later!
How do I read and interpret pivot charts? They seem confusing. ‚ñ∂

Pivot charts look complex at first, but once you understand the components, they become powerful analysis tools!

üîç Key Components

  1. The main chart area: Shows your data visually (bars, lines, etc.)
  2. Axes:
    • Horizontal (X): Categories (e.g., months, products)
    • Vertical (Y): Values (e.g., sales amounts, quantities)
  3. Legend: Explains what different colors/patterns represent
  4. Filter buttons: Small dropdown arrows - click to filter data

üìñ Reading Strategy

  1. Look at the title: What is being measured?
  2. Check the axes: What do the numbers represent?
  3. Examine the legend: What do the colors mean?
  4. Identify patterns:
    • Which bars are tallest/shortest?
    • Are there trends (going up/down)?
    • Any outliers or surprises?

Interacting with Pivot Charts:

  1. Click filter buttons (small dropdowns on the chart) to show/hide specific data
  2. Hover over chart elements to see exact values
  3. Click legend items to show/hide specific series
  4. Change the underlying pivot table and the chart updates automatically
Pro tip: The power of pivot charts is in the exploration. Try clicking different filters and watch how the visualization changes. This helps you discover insights you might miss in raw data!
Common question: "Why does my chart look weird?" Usually it's because:
  • Wrong chart type for your data (try a different type)
  • Too many categories (filter to show fewer)
  • Data in Values area should be in Rows or vice versa (rearrange in pivot table)
Can I add a pivot chart to a Word document or PowerPoint presentation? ‚ñ∂

Yes! You can easily move charts between Office applications. Here's how:

Method 1: Copy and Paste (Simple, but Chart Becomes Static)

  1. In Excel: Click on your pivot chart to select it
  2. Copy: Ctrl+C (Windows) or Cmd+C (Mac)
  3. Switch to Word/PowerPoint
  4. Paste: Ctrl+V (Windows) or Cmd+V (Mac)
  5. Choose paste option:
    • Picture: Chart becomes an image (can't edit)
    • Keep Source Formatting: Maintains Excel formatting
    • Use Destination Theme: Adapts to Word/PowerPoint theme

Method 2: Paste with Link (Chart Updates When Excel Data Changes)

  1. In Excel: Select and copy your chart (Ctrl+C / Cmd+C)
  2. In Word/PowerPoint: Go to Home tab
  3. Click the dropdown arrow under Paste button
  4. Choose "Paste Special"
  5. Select "Paste Link" and choose "Microsoft Excel Chart Object"
  6. Click OK

üìã Static Paste

Pros:

  • File size smaller
  • No need to keep Excel file
  • Guaranteed to look the same

Cons:

  • Won't update if data changes
  • Have to recreate if you need changes

Best for: Final reports, presentations where data won't change

üîó Linked Paste

Pros:

  • Updates automatically when Excel data changes
  • Always shows current data
  • Can edit by double-clicking

Cons:

  • Requires keeping Excel file
  • Link can break if files move
  • Larger file size

Best for: Living documents, recurring reports

Important: If you use paste with link, keep your Excel file in the same location! If you move it, the link will break and the chart won't update.
Pro tip: For presentations, paste as a picture for the cleanest look. For reports that you'll update regularly, use paste link so your charts automatically refresh!

Excel keeps crashing or freezing on my computer. What can I do? ‚ñ∂

Excel crashes can be frustrating! Here are the most common causes and solutions:

Quick Fixes to Try First:

  1. Save and restart Excel: Close completely and reopen
  2. Restart your computer: Clears memory and background processes
  3. Check for updates:
    • Windows: File ‚Üí Account ‚Üí Update Options ‚Üí Update Now
    • Mac: Help ‚Üí Check for Updates
  4. Disable add-ins: File ‚Üí Options ‚Üí Add-ins ‚Üí Manage: COM Add-ins ‚Üí Go ‚Üí Uncheck all ‚Üí OK

üêå If Excel is Slow/Freezing

Common causes:

  • File is too large (many thousands of rows)
  • Too many formulas recalculating
  • Conditional formatting on large ranges
  • Links to external files

Solutions:

  • Turn off automatic calculation: Formulas tab ‚Üí Calculation Options ‚Üí Manual
  • Remove unnecessary formatting
  • Split large files into smaller ones
  • Close other programs to free up memory

üí• If Excel Keeps Crashing

Try these solutions:

  • Open in Safe Mode:
    • Windows: Hold Ctrl while opening Excel
    • Mac: Hold Shift while opening
  • Repair Office:
    • Windows: Control Panel ‚Üí Programs ‚Üí Repair
    • Mac: Reinstall from App Store or office.com
  • Check if specific file causes crashes (file corruption)
Important: Always save your work frequently! Use Ctrl+S (Cmd+S on Mac) regularly, or enable AutoSave if using OneDrive or SharePoint.

How did you get ChatGPT to give you what you wanted in Excel? ‚ñ∂

If you were in one of my (Prof Haas') classes, you may have seen me paste the column of favorite movie titles into ChatGPT and ask it to generate a new column that provided a genre for each movie title.

The purpose was to categorize every title without having to go row by row. Of course, I'm letting the AI assign the genre, so there might be times when it predicts a genre that is not suitable for the title.

ü§ñ Understanding Generative AI

What results you get depend on several factors!
  1. Training data/bias: Anthropic, Google, Microsoct, and OpenAI are U.S.-based companies. I'd bet ChatGPT's training data featured more info about popular American movies!
  2. Prompt features: Later in class, we'll learn about the "CLEAR" method for crafting effective prompts. GenAI is very sensitive to phrasing and does better when we give it examples.
  3. Iteration: Core to computational thinking, when we don't get exactly the right output the 1st time, we construct feedback and get more specific about what we do or DON'T want.
      Example:
    • I asked ChatGPT to print the Genre column again beside each movie title so I could compare accross. Asking for a table format may also make it easier to copy into Excel.

ü§ñ Understanding Generative AI

The transformer architecture that generative AI is built on was trained to get really good at predicting the next word. GPT-1 was trained on massive amounts of text data to recognize patterns and predict the next coherent word at a time. Once OpenAI had GPT-1 (circa 2018) trained on the structure and patterns of human language, they began fine-tuning their model for specific, single tasks like text summarization and question answering.

GPT-2 in 2019 was expoentially larger than GPT-1 consisting of 1.5 billion parameters -- small by 2025 standards (for reference, an M4 MacBook Air can easily run 4- to 8-billion-parameter models, and models like Claude Sonnet 4.5 are estimated to be have 150-250 billion parameters). It was handling open-ended prompts for a variety of lanugage tasks, but still lacked factual accuracy and sruggled with long conversations.

GPT-3 in 2020 was a giant leap from 1.5 to 175 billion parameters! Prior to the groundbreaking 2020 paper "Scaling Laws for Neural Language Models" by Kaplan et al., it was not widely assumed that there was a predictable power law between model "loss", dataset size, and computing power. The discovery of this law allowed for optimization of training and showed the promise of training large models with fewer steps (as opposed to small models with many steps). GPT-3 provided capable of handling a much wider array of tasks fluently (writing essays, coding from a single prompt), but it suffered more pronounced hallucinations and lack of contextual memory for long conversations.

Generative AI models have tended to become more capable since (albeit with more potent side-effects and dangers along the way, notably sycophancy). Keep in mind, the that fundumaentally, we are still dealing will fallable prediction machines. Ask ChatGPT to write you a paper on a topic of your choice and cite its sources. Check those sources very carefully! There's a strong chance that it cited real sources but those sources don't actually make the claim or support the statements that it attributes to them. There's also a strong chance it wrote some sources based on real authors and keywords in the titles of papers related to your topic that aren't actually real sources!
