# The Connected World — Study Guide

## 1. How the Internet Works

The internet is often compared to a postal service. Think about what that analogy means: what plays the role of the address? What plays the role of the delivery person? What plays the role of the post office itself?

When you type a URL into your browser, a sequence of events kicks off involving your device, your router, your ISP, and a remote server. Make sure you can describe that sequence in your own words — from the moment you hit Enter to the moment the page loads.

**Ask yourself:**
- What is a server, and why do large websites use many of them?
- What is the difference between a local IP address and a public IP address?
- Why do devices on the same home network share one public IP address?
- What role does the router play in managing traffic between your devices and the internet?
- What does ISP stand for, and what is its function?

---

## 2. Bandwidth

Bandwidth is often visualized as a pipe — the wider the pipe, the more data flows through at once.

**Ask yourself:**
- What unit is bandwidth typically measured in?
- What is the difference between download speed and upload speed?
- Why do most ISPs offer asymmetric bandwidth plans?
- What is throttling, and when might an ISP do it?
- How does fiber optic cable differ from copper wire in terms of how data travels?

---

## 3. Why Networks Slow Down

There are several distinct reasons a network can feel slow. Try to distinguish between problems that originate in *your home*, problems that originate with *your ISP*, and problems that originate on the *server side*.

**Ask yourself:**
- How does the number of connected devices affect your experience?
- Why does distance from your router matter, and does moving up or down a floor have a different effect than moving sideways?
- What is the trade-off between the 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz Wi-Fi bands?
- What is server congestion, and what causes it?
- What are background apps doing that can contribute to a slow network?

---

## 4. Data Packets and Streaming

Data doesn't travel across the internet in one big chunk — it's broken into smaller pieces. Understanding this explains why streaming works the way it does.

**Ask yourself:**
- What is a data packet?
- How does a streaming service like Netflix deliver video to your device?
- What is buffering, and why does it happen?
- What is packet loss?

---

## 5. Online Gaming and Ping

Gamers care a lot about a specific network metric. Make sure you understand what it measures and what affects it.

**Ask yourself:**
- What is ping, and is a higher or lower value better?
- Why does the physical location of a game server matter to a player?
- What happens during the matchmaking process before a game starts?
- What is one practical step a gamer can take to improve their connection stability?

---

## 6. Cell Networks and 5G

Mobile networking has evolved through several generations. You should be familiar with the general trend and what 5G represents.

**Ask yourself:**
- What does the "G" stand for in 2G, 3G, 4G, and 5G?
- How does speed compare across generations?
- Why does 5G require more physical infrastructure than 4G?
- What is millimeter wave bandwidth, and why is 5G coverage uneven between urban and rural areas?
- What is one emerging technology that 5G could make significantly more prevalent?

**Myth check:** A common misconception exists about 5G and health. What does the electromagnetic spectrum tell us about the energy level of 5G signals compared to other things we're already exposed to?

---

## 7. Online Payments

When you tap your card to pay, a lot happens in a short time. Think through the chain of communication involved.

**Ask yourself:**
- What happens to your card data before it's transmitted?
- Who are the different parties involved in processing a payment (hint: there are at least three)?
- Why does data need to be encrypted both *in transit* and *at rest*?
- What should happen to decrypted data after it's been used?
